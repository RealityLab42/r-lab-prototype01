AR menus that sell themselves.

An augmented menu for [Client], where every dish appears on the table before it's ordered.
See it in your space

Drag to turn the dish. On a phone, tap to place it on your table at full size.

Braised short rib — one of fourteen dishes captured for the menu.
The problem

Photographs flatten food. Descriptions oversell it. Diners order blind and restaurants absorb the disappointment — in sent-back plates, in reviews, in the dishes nobody ever tries.

In use

Fourteen dishes, photogrammetrically captured and rebuilt as three-dimensional models diners can see at actual scale on their own table.

Filmed at [Location].
▶
The outcome

The menu became the most-photographed object in the room. Servers stopped describing dishes and started handing over the phone.

I began my creative life as a writer, and I still think like one.

I've also always been an avid science fiction reader — which means I've always been interested in technology, and specifically in how technology changes the way we tell a story.

Gutenberg's moveable type made the novel possible. That's the pattern I keep coming back to: a new technology arrives, and it doesn't just carry old stories more efficiently. It makes new kinds of stories possible.

So I've spent thirty-five years arriving early. Early CD-ROM. Early web — Wheeler Design Group was one of the first all-digital design firms in Boston. Online magazines and stores before that was a category. A news marketplace built to connect readers directly to journalists. And now augmented reality, which is the most interesting storytelling technology to arrive in my working life.

— J. Michael Wheeler
Reality Lab®

In January 2020 we pivoted Wheeler Design Group into Reality Lab, and pointed twenty-five years of strategy and design straight at augmented reality.

We conceive, design, and deploy AR experiences for business, art, and communication. Menus that let diners see the dish. Exhibitions that put the invisible in the room. Environmental work that shows people a future they'd otherwise have to imagine.

How we work

I've always used what I call the Movie Studio Model. I don't keep a staff of specialists — I assemble a team for each project, built around what that project actually needs.

The reason is simple. If I employed a videographer, I'd have to sell video, whether or not video was the right answer. Instead I hire the solution. That's how we can look at any problem and give you an honest opinion about whether AR is the right tool — and if it is, what kind.

AR Ain't Video

Everyone who commissions their first AR piece asks for a storyboard. Here's why that's the wrong instinct — and what to ask for instead.

The first thing a new client hands me is almost always a storyboard. Frames, arrows, a beginning and an end. It's what you make when you've spent your career thinking in video, and it's the single clearest sign that we haven't started the real conversation yet.

Video is a river. It runs one direction, at one speed, and every viewer stands in the same spot. You control the frame because you control everything — where the eye goes, when the cut lands, how long the moment lasts. That control is the whole art form.

AR isn't a river. It's a room. You don't control where the viewer stands — you decide what's worth walking toward.

Once you accept that, the storyboard stops being useful and starts being a cage. The right question isn't "what happens first, then next." It's "what does someone find when they look here, and what pulls them to look there."

A storyboard frame beside a spatial map of the same piece. One is a sequence; the other is a place.

This is why our first deliverable is rarely a script. It's a space — blocked out, walkable, empty of content but full of intent. The story comes after we know the room.
